NHSN Evidence

Expand a category to review the 2026 NHSN criteria supplied for this tool.

Superficial Incisional SSI

Must meet all of the following

  1. Date of event occurs within 30 days following the NHSN operative procedure, where day 1 is the procedure date.
  2. Involves only skin and subcutaneous tissue of the incision.
  3. Patient has at least one of the following:
    1. Purulent drainage from the superficial incision.
    2. Organism(s) identified from an aseptically obtained specimen from the superficial incision or subcutaneous tissue by a culture- or non-culture-based microbiologic testing method performed for clinical diagnosis or treatment.
    3. A superficial incision deliberately opened, re-accessed, or aspirated by a surgeon, physician, or physician designee; and antibiotic or antifungal therapy is initiated or continued on or in the two calendar days following the procedure, for two calendar days or longer; and the patient has new or worsening localized pain or tenderness, localized swelling, erythema, or heat.
    4. Diagnosis of a superficial incisional SSI by a physician or physician designee.

Comments

  • SIP: Superficial Incisional Primary — identified in the primary incision.
  • SIS: Superficial Incisional Secondary — identified in a secondary incision when the operation has more than one incision.

Does not qualify by itself

  • Diagnosis or treatment of cellulitis alone.
  • A stitch abscess alone.
  • A localized stab wound or pin-site infection.

Important note

For NHSN surveillance, a laparoscopic trocar site is considered a surgical incision rather than a stab wound. A trocar site used for drain placement at the end of the procedure is considered a surgical incision.

Deep Incisional SSI

Must meet all of the following

  1. Date of event occurs within 30 or 90 days following the NHSN operative procedure, according to the applicable procedure surveillance period.
  2. Involves deep soft tissues of the incision, such as fascial and muscle layers.
  3. Patient has at least one of the following:
    1. Purulent drainage from the deep incision.
    2. Organism(s) identified from the deep soft tissues of the incision by a culture- or non-culture-based microbiologic testing method performed for clinical diagnosis or treatment.
    3. A deep incision deliberately opened, re-accessed, or aspirated by a surgeon, physician, or physician designee, or spontaneously dehisces; and antibiotic or antifungal therapy is initiated or continued on or in the two calendar days following that event, for two calendar days or longer; and the patient has fever >38°C or new/worsening localized pain or tenderness.
    4. An abscess or other evidence of infection involving the deep incision detected on gross anatomic examination, histopathologic examination, or imaging test.

Definitions and comments

  • Spontaneous dehiscence is a re-opening of a surgical incision not caused by an external factor such as direct trauma.
  • DIP: Deep Incisional Primary — identified in the primary incision.
  • DIS: Deep Incisional Secondary — identified in a secondary incision when the operation has more than one incision.
Organ/Space SSI

Must meet all of the following

  1. Date of event occurs within 30 or 90 days following the NHSN operative procedure, according to the applicable procedure surveillance period.
  2. Involves organ/space tissues deeper than the fascia or muscle layers.
  3. Patient has at least one of the following:
    1. Purulent drainage from a drain placed into the organ/space, such as a closed suction drainage system, open drain, T-tube drain, or CT-guided drainage system.
    2. Organism(s) identified from fluid or tissue in the organ/space by a culture- or non-culture-based microbiologic testing method performed for clinical diagnosis or treatment.
    3. An abscess or other evidence of infection involving the organ/space detected on:
      • gross anatomic examination,
      • histopathologic examination, or
      • imaging test evidence definitive or equivocal for infection.
  4. Meets at least one eligible criterion for a specific organ/space infection site in the NHSN site-specific definitions.

Examples of gross anatomic evidence

  • An intraabdominal abscess generally requires an invasive procedure to directly visualize the abscess.
  • Visualization of pus or purulent drainage, including drainage from a drain.
  • Special reproductive-tract provisions may apply after certain procedures when the appropriate site-specific definition is met.

COLO site-specific classification

  • GIT: Gastrointestinal tract.
  • IAB: Intraabdominal infection, not specified elsewhere.
  • OREP: Other reproductive tract infection.

References

  • CDC NHSN Patient Safety Component Manual, Chapter 9: Surgical Site Infection Event.
  • CDC NHSN Patient Safety Component Manual, Chapter 17: Surveillance Definitions for Specific Types of Infections.
  • Evidence text in this tool should be reviewed annually against the current NHSN manual.
